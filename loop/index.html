One real arc

The loop, catching itself

Every agent demo shows the happy path. This is the opposite: a real piece of work from my harness where the reviewer refused the builder's report, proved it wrong from source, and blocked the change. The builder and the reviewer are both me — or rather, both the loop. Click through the five stages.

Nothing here is reconstructed for the demo. It happened, in that order, on my own machine.

The point isn't that the loop is clever. It's that the reviewer didn't believe the report — it went and checked.

That's the same principle as the differential oracle: never accept one account of the truth when you can derive a second one independently and make them agree. There, it's two implementations of the same logic. Here, it's a report's arithmetic versus the file it describes. A claim that can't survive an independent re-derivation isn't evidence — it's just confident.

Why the reviewer runs cold. It gets a fresh context — it never watched the work being done, so it has nothing invested in the story the builder tells. A reviewer who sat through the build inherits the builder's assumptions, and the one assumption in this arc that needed challenging was the one the builder was most confident about.

What's here and what isn't. This is a narration, not a document dump. The loop's actual artifacts, formats, gate definitions and doctrine stay private — that's the machinery, and it's the part that took months. What's shown is the story and two lines from the review, which is all the evidence needs. If you want the runnable version of this same principle, the oracle is public and executes in your browser.
